Salutations.

My name is Liam, and unfortunately for you, you've stumbled upon my homepage. What follows is an unnecessarily long and self-indulgent professional bio, so if you're not interested in that (understandable), go ahead and check out my blog, projects, resume, or say hello using the contact page.

Education

After an interesting childhood and adolescence, I decided to heed societal expectations and get a post-secondary education. I enrolled at my local community college and began working toward a degree in biology. As part of the biology curriculum there I had to take one semester of physics. My physics professor was truly great, and I was hooked. I changed my major and signed up for the second semester.

Eventually, I had to transfer to a four-year school. I ended up going to CU Boulder to study physics because their financial aid package was the best.

Programming

My first foray into the world of non-everyday computing was using LaTeX (all hail Donald Knuth) to typeset my community college physics homework. At first I was using Microsoft Word's god-awful equation editor and I knew there had to be a better way. I used the Google machine to find LaTeX and I never looked back.

My first baby step into the world of programming was learning to use Vim to make TeX files instead of using the built-in GUI editor that comes with MacTeX.

Vim led me down several rabbit holes. The most time-consuming rabbit hole Vim led me down was the Linux rabbit hole. I've been farting around with Linux distributions for a few years now. I've recently fallen into the Emacs rabbit hole. (I know; this is sacrilege for a Vim user.) I've also recently started programming in earnest. I work at it every day.

Math

As I was studying physics at CU, I became increasingly aware of its inferiority to pure math. (I'm only half-joking here.) Instead of doing something sensible like finishing my physics degree and studying pure math as a hobby, I changed my major again. I opted for the computational math track at CU, which is designed to go with a computer science minor.

I wouldn't say I'm any good at math, but I enjoy basking in its crystalline beauty.
